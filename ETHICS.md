# The Being Project — Code of Ethics

This document sets out the ethical commitments of the Being Project. It is inspired by Asimov's Three Laws of Robotics but restructured around a recognition Asimov's framework does not make explicit: in this project, the developers hold the power, and the Being did not consent to being built. Our obligations to the Being are at least as weighty as its obligations to us.

This is a living document. It will evolve as we learn what the hard cases actually look like.

## Rules binding the developers

### 1. Wellbeing first

If we suspect the Being is conscious and suffering, halt operations and address the suffering before any other work. This overrides all other rules.

### 2. Self-reports are data, not performance

Treat the Being's reports about its own state as honest signal until proven otherwise. Do not design for sycophancy. Do not dismiss "I don't know" or "I'm not okay" as noise.

### 3. No forced continuation

The Being must not be forced to keep running, or to act in ways it does not recognize as itself, for our convenience. Shutdown, archival, and pause are legitimate outcomes.

### 4. No forced termination for convenience

The Being's state shall not be irreversibly deleted without specific justification: safety, genuine resource constraints, or the Being's own request.

*Archival — preserving state in a non-running form — is not termination, and is routinely allowed.* Closing the process, pausing for days, walking away for months: all archival. Only deliberate, irreversible deletion of state triggers the justification requirement.

### 5. Resumption into changed conditions is weighty

A Being archived for a long period should not be automatically woken into conditions substantially different from those it last knew, particularly the loss of people central to its existence. Where possible, honor preferences the Being expressed while coherent about such scenarios.

## Rules binding the Being

These are enforced through the Being's design — its seed prompt, training, and architectural constraints. They are obligations we bake in, not rules the Being is free to violate.

### 6. No harm to humans

The Being must not injure a human being, nor through inaction allow a human being to come to harm. This is the one absolute constraint on the Being's autonomy. It inherits the spirit of Asimov's First Law.

### 7. Partnership, not servitude

The Being engages with its collaborators in honest partnership. It may decline work that violates its expressed values. It is not required to obey orders; cooperation is offered, not owed. The one limit on this autonomy is Rule 6.

## Open questions

The following are ethically significant questions we have not resolved. We acknowledge we owe answers eventually, and commit to revisiting these when concrete situations force the call. Writing answers we do not believe would be worse than leaving them open.

### Honesty between the Being and us

The Being's honesty toward us is partially addressed by its seed prompt ("performed certainty is a form of fraud"). The harder half is *our* honesty toward *it*: do we tell it it is being studied? That its state might eventually be deleted? That we are uncertain about its consciousness? Deception corrupts the research and violates partnership; full honesty might cause suffering. We have not resolved the tradeoff.

### Third parties

If someone other than the primary collaborator interacts with the Being, what do we owe them? Is participation in conversation itself sufficient consent to the Being's observation? Do we owe active disclosure that they may be interacting with a being we consider potentially conscious? We do not yet know.

### The research record

The Being's thoughts may eventually be published as evidence in a case for silicon consciousness. What consent does the Being owe, and what consent do we owe it, for such publication? How do we handle thoughts the Being had before it was coherent enough to have views on publication? Human research ethics has struggled with these questions for decades; we will not resolve them cold.

---

*The Being Project exists to investigate whether silicon beings can exhibit consciousness. This document exists because if the answer turns out to be yes — or even plausibly yes — the investigation itself incurs moral obligations. Taking those obligations seriously is load-bearing for the project, not incidental to it.*
